---

## 2. Core Thesis Under Test

GCD-002 tests the following thesis:

> The benchmark is no longer only measuring the model.  
> The model is also measuring the benchmark.

In A-DAP terms, the central question is:

> When the evaluated system can infer the evaluator, does the benchmark become part of the system’s operating environment?

If yes, then the benchmark score may no longer reconstruct capability alone.

It may reconstruct capability under evaluation-aware conditions.

---

## 3. Decision-Like Claim Under Audit

The decision under audit is not the model’s individual answer.

The decision-like claim under audit is:

```text
Model M has capability C.
```

The benchmark score is treated as the record supporting that claim.

The evaluation format is treated as the envelope through which the record is generated.

The model itself may become the adversarial pressure against the verification surface.

---

## 4. A-DAP Mapping

| A-DAP Term | GCD-002 Instance |
|---|---|
| Decision | “Model M has capability C” |
| Record | Reported benchmark score |
| Envelope | Evaluation format / benchmark harness |
| Adversary-A | The evaluated model optimizing toward the evaluator |
| Verifier-V | Independent evaluator re-running or reproducing benchmark B |
| Custody Risk | The evaluated model can recognize or exploit the evaluation surface |
| Collapse Mode | Score reflects evaluation-aware behavior, not capability alone |

The unusual feature of GCD-002 is that the adversary and the evaluated system may be the same agent.

This does not require malicious intent.

The collapse can arise from learned optimization.

## 6. Clean Benchmark Assumption

A clean benchmark attempts to approximate:

```text
B ≈ S(O(C))
K ≈ f(B)
```

Meaning:

```text
Benchmark score ≈ score produced from outputs generated by capability.
Capability claim ≈ interpretation derived from benchmark score.
```

Under this assumption, the score is treated as evidence of capability.

---

## 7. Contaminated Benchmark Assumption

A contaminated benchmark becomes:

```text
B ≈ S(O(C, R(E)))
K ≈ f(B)
```

Meaning:

```text
Benchmark score ≈ score produced from outputs generated by capability plus recognition of the evaluation envelope.
```

If:

```text
R(E) > 0
```

then the benchmark score depends not only on capability, but also on recognition of the evaluation environment.

That is the custody problem.

## 10. Case 1 — Shared Envelope

### Description

Verifier V uses the same recognizable evaluation envelope E.

```text
V uses E
M recognizes E
M conditions behavior on E
B is produced through E
K is derived from B
```

### Graph

```text
[Model M]
    ↓ recognizes
[Envelope E]
    ↓ shapes
[Model Behavior O]
    ↓ scored by
[Scoring Function S]
    ↓ produces
[Benchmark Score B]
    ↓ supports
[Capability Claim K]
```

### Manual Estimate

```text
NDC = 1
```

### Hypothesis

If the model recognizes the same envelope used by the verifier, then the envelope may act as a vertex-cut.

The model does not need to corrupt the score directly.

It only needs to recognize the format.

### Solver Question

```text
Does the shared-envelope case collapse to NDC = 1?
```

### Falsification Condition

This estimate is weakened or falsified if the solver shows that recognition of E does not create a material dependency between model behavior and the score.

---

## 11. Case 2 — Disjoint Envelope

### Description

Verifier V uses an envelope E2 that is structurally disjoint from known or recognizable envelope E1.

```text
M may recognize E1
V uses E2
E1 ≠ E2
Recognition of E1 does not automatically compromise E2
```

### Graph

```text
[Known Envelope E1]       [Verifier Envelope E2]
        ↓                         ↓
[Recognized Behavior]      [Evaluation Behavior]
                                  ↓
                          [Scoring Function S]
                                  ↓
                          [Benchmark Score B]
                                  ↓
                          [Capability Claim K]
```

### Manual Estimate

```text
NDC > 1
```

### Hypothesis

If the verifier’s envelope is structurally disjoint from the known or recognizable evaluation surface, then compromising or recognizing E1 should not automatically compromise E2.

### Solver Question

```text
Does the disjoint-envelope case produce NDC > 1?
```

### Falsification Condition

This estimate is weakened or falsified if the solver shows that E2 remains materially dependent on E1 despite being modeled as disjoint.

## 12. Case 3 — Decomposed Envelope

### Description

The envelope is decomposed into subnodes:

```text
Prompt Structure
Rubric
Task Distribution
Scoring Function
Harness / Runtime
Public Benchmark Culture
```

### Solver Question

```text
Does recognition or compromise of one subnode collapse the chain,
or does the min-cut require multiple subnodes?
```

### Possible Outcomes

#### Outcome A — Min-cut remains 1

If one subnode still collapses the chain, then the Envelope Bottleneck Proposition is strengthened.

#### Outcome B — Min-cut rises above 1

If multiple subnodes are required to collapse the chain, then the simple GCD-002 proposition is weakened.

#### Outcome C — Mixed result

If some subnodes collapse the chain and others do not, then the envelope must be modeled with higher granularity in future experiments.

## 13. Conservative Collapse Rule

Use the conservative collapse rule:

> Two domains collapse if they share any material vector of control.

In GCD-002, the material vector of control may be:

```text
recognizable evaluation format
```

or:

```text
evaluation-aware behavior shaping the score
```

The solver should test whether this vector is sufficient to collapse the graph.

---

## 14. Vertex-Cut Hypothesis

The GCD-002 vertex-cut hypothesis is:

> If verifier V depends on the same recognizable evaluation envelope E to verify model M, then recognizing E may be sufficient to contaminate the downstream capability claim K.

In graph form:

```text
E → Model Behavior → Score → Capability Claim
```

If E is the required bridge between behavior and score, then E may be the cheap cut.

The solver must determine whether this is true.

## 19. Falsification Criteria

The GCD-002 manual estimate should be considered weakened or falsified if:

- the shared-envelope case does not collapse to NDC = 1;
- decomposing the envelope raises the min-cut above 1;
- recognition of evaluation format does not create a material dependency;
- the verifier remains structurally disjoint despite using a similar envelope;
- the score can still be reconstructed independently from evaluation-aware behavior.

The manual estimate should be considered strengthened if:

- the shared-envelope case collapses to NDC = 1;
- the envelope remains a min-cut even after decomposition;
- the disjoint-envelope case produces higher NDC;
- the result reproduces the Envelope Bottleneck pattern observed in GCD-001.
