# IP Priority and Authorized Execution — What A-DAP Can and Cannot Prove

## Status

Open architectural design note.

This document defines the defensible use of A-DAP for intellectual-property-adjacent evidence.

It rejects algorithmic plagiarism detection based on behavioral similarity, output correlation, or alleged “algorithmic DNA.”

A-DAP can support two narrower evidentiary claims:

1. temporal priority evidence;
2. externally authorized execution evidence.

It cannot prove algorithmic plagiarism from output similarity alone.

It cannot prove authorized execution from a self-signed receipt generated by the same engine whose authorization is being questioned.

---

## 1. Core Claim

A-DAP can help prove that a specific artifact existed in a specific form no later than a specific time.

A-DAP can also help show that a specific execution presented an externally issued authorization token valid for a declared version, scope, or time window.

But A-DAP cannot prove that a third party copied an algorithm merely because its outputs, behavior, or decision structure appear similar.

Similarity is not origin.

Correlation is not authorship.

A Merkle tree proves integrity of committed data.

It does not prove authorship of a function.

A hash proves correspondence to a committed artifact.

It does not prove that another party did not independently arrive at a similar method.

---

## 2. Rejected Use Case: Algorithmic Plagiarism Detection

The following claim is unsafe:

“This system can detect algorithmic plagiarism by comparing behavioral fingerprints, output correlation, or decision patterns.”

This is not defensible.

Two independent systems may produce similar outputs because they solve the same problem well.

A third party may implement a similar method without copying code.

A high correlation between outputs may indicate functional similarity, but it does not establish origin.

It may support investigation.

It may support expert review.

It may support a legal theory when combined with access, copying evidence, code similarity, contractual breach, or other facts.

But it is not proof by itself.

A-DAP should not become an automated plagiarism oracle.

---

## 3. Why “Algorithmic DNA” Is Not Proof

The phrase “algorithmic DNA” is misleading if it implies recoverable authorship from behavior.

A decision system may expose:

- similar outputs,
- similar rankings,
- similar thresholds,
- similar error patterns,
- similar confidence distributions,
- similar policy choices,
- similar trace structure.

None of these alone proves copying.

They may result from:

- common training data,
- common domain constraints,
- public methods,
- shared optimization targets,
- standard engineering practice,
- convergent design,
- regulatory requirements,
- or independent implementation.

A-DAP should not treat behavioral resemblance as cryptographic evidence.

The correct category is not proof.

The correct category is investigative signal.

---

## 4. Valid Use Case 1 — Temporal Priority

A-DAP can support temporal priority.

Temporal priority means:

“This artifact existed in this form no later than this date.”

A defensible temporal-priority record may include:

- source artifact hash,
- release bundle hash,
- model card hash,
- specification hash,
- schema hash,
- governance document hash,
- signed manifest,
- release tag,
- commit hash,
- RFC 3161 timestamp token,
- external ledger anchor,
- reproducibility instructions.

The safe claim is:

“This artifact existed in this committed form no later than the timestamp or anchor date, under the stated custody assumptions.”

This is useful.

It helps establish chronology.

It can support provenance disputes.

It can support legal counsel.

It can support licensing records.

It can support prior-art or authorship timelines.

But it has a strict limit.

---

## 5. Temporal Priority Is Not Sole Authorship

Temporal priority does not prove sole authorship.

It does not prove that nobody else independently created a similar artifact.

It does not prove that a later similar artifact copied from this one.

It does not prove that the timestamped party was the original author in an absolute sense.

It proves only existence-by-date under stated assumptions.

If another party can show an earlier timestamped artifact, an independent development record, or a parallel creation path, the A-DAP timestamp does not defeat that by itself.

Therefore, the safe formulation is:

“This artifact was committed by this party in this form no later than this date.”

The unsafe formulation is:

“This timestamp proves that any later similar system copied this artifact.”

That is false.

---

## 6. Valid Use Case 2 — Authorized Execution

A-DAP can support authorized execution evidence, but only under a narrower architecture.

The unsafe claim is:

“This decision was produced by an authorized deployment because the engine signed its own receipt.”

This is self-attestation.

If the deployment being checked controls the key or mechanism that signs “authorized,” then the adversary controls the authorization claim.

A licensed operator who exceeds scope may:

- generate false “authorized” receipts;
- suppress receipts entirely;
- copy a valid license key to an undeclared deployment;
- run the engine outside the approved environment;
- backdate or replay authorization claims;
- issue receipts only for compliant executions.

If the engine attests to its own authorization, NDC collapses.

The authorization claim must not be generated solely by the actor whose compliance is being tested.

---

## 7. Externally Issued Authorization Token

A defensible authorized-execution design requires an authorization token issued by a materially disjoint authority.

The safe claim is:

“This execution presents an authorization token issued by a disjoint authorization authority, valid for the declared version, scope, deployment, and time window.”

This does not prove the decision was correct.

It does not prove the license was never abused.

It does not prove all executions were recorded.

It does not prove the operator was honest.

It proves only that this execution contains a verifiable authorization artifact issued outside the deployment being checked.

A valid token may bind:

- license identifier,
- deployment identifier,
- model or engine version,
- allowed scope,
- time window,
- execution quota,
- customer or tenant identifier,
- environment identifier,
- public key,
- policy version,
- receipt schema,
- nonce or challenge,
- revocation status,
- token issuer identity.

The token must be verifiable after the fact.

---

## 8. Authorization Token Control Vector

The key architectural question is:

Who controls the authorization token issuer?

If the licensee controls the issuer, the authorization claim collapses.

If the operator controls the issuer, the authorization claim collapses.

If the engine controls the issuer, the authorization claim collapses.

If the issuer is disjoint, auditable, and independently reachable, the authorization path may add an independent constraint.

The relevant safe claim is:

“The cost of falsifying authorization is bounded by the NDC of the authorization issuer and its custody path.”

Not:

“The system proves authorized execution absolutely.”

The token issuer itself must be included in the custody graph.

---

## 9. Online Authorization Trade-Off

Externally issued authorization creates a trade-off.

If each execution requires a fresh online token, then the authorization path may be stronger, but the system gains an availability dependency.

The token issuer may become a critical runtime dependency.

This can create operational risk:

- outage risk,
- latency risk,
- denial-of-service risk,
- censorship risk,
- dependency on issuer availability,
- incompatibility with air-gapped deployments.

If tokens are issued in batches or windows, the system reduces runtime dependency but creates an authorization window.

Inside that window, abuse may occur until detected later.

Therefore, authorized execution can be implemented in several modes:

### 9.1 Per-Execution Token

Strongest linkage to each execution.

Highest runtime dependency.

### 9.2 Windowed Token

Authorization valid for a fixed time window.

Lower runtime dependency.

Some abuse may occur within the window.

### 9.3 Quota Token

Authorization valid for a defined number of executions.

Useful for licensed usage limits.

Requires reliable counting or receipt reconciliation.

### 9.4 Offline Token

Supports air-gapped or disconnected deployments.

Weaker real-time enforcement.

Requires later reconciliation.

None of these eliminates trust.

Each shifts the trust boundary.

---

## 10. Absence of Token

The absence of an authorization token is not automatic proof of unauthorized execution.

It may indicate:

- unauthorized execution;
- missing receipt;
- broken integration;
- offline mode;
- logging failure;
- token issuer outage;
- unsupported legacy deployment;
- incomplete evidence.

The safe claim is:

“No valid authorization token was presented for this execution under the declared verification procedure.”

The unsafe claim is:

“This execution was definitely unauthorized.”

That conclusion may require legal, contractual, or institutional review.

---

## 11. Suppressed Receipts

Authorized-execution receipts only help when receipts exist.

An adversarial licensee may run the engine while suppressing receipt generation.

A-DAP can detect missing receipts only if there is an independent expectation of receipt completeness.

That may require:

- metered execution,
- external usage counters,
- reconciliation logs,
- independent telemetry,
- customer-side receipt collection,
- regulator-side receipt submission,
- contractual reporting duties,
- audit rights,
- or external token issuance records.

Without an independent completeness mechanism, A-DAP may verify presented receipts while missing unreported executions.

This is not a cryptographic flaw.

It is a coverage and enforcement problem.

---

## 12. Replayed or Copied Tokens

A valid token may be copied or replayed unless it is bound to context.

Authorization tokens should be bound where possible to:

- deployment key,
- execution nonce,
- time window,
- model version,
- license scope,
- environment identifier,
- receipt hash,
- or challenge-response exchange.

If a token is portable across unauthorized deployments, it becomes a weak control.

The token must not merely say:

“License valid.”

It should say:

“This license authorization was issued for this declared context.”

---

## 13. Relation to NDC

Authorized execution is meaningful only if the authorization path adds an independent constraint.

If the same actor controls:

- the engine,
- the receipt,
- the signing key,
- the license token,
- the verification portal,
- and the usage log,

then apparent authorization evidence collapses.

Under conservative NDC analysis, the authorization path may remain NDC = 1.

To improve independence, the authorization issuer and verification path must be materially disjoint from the licensee whose compliance is being tested.

---

## 14. Relation to A-DAP

A-DAP can support IP-adjacent governance by producing reconstructible evidence around:

- release chronology,
- artifact existence,
- version custody,
- license scope,
- execution receipts,
- authorization tokens,
- deployment identity,
- and reconciliation logs.

But it must not claim to solve:

- algorithmic plagiarism,
- independent invention disputes,
- copyright infringement by behavior alone,
- method protection,
- idea protection,
- or authorship from output similarity.

A-DAP is evidence infrastructure.

It is not an authorship oracle.

---

## 15. Safe Claims

Safe claims include:

“This artifact existed in this committed form no later than this date.”

“This release bundle matches the hash anchored on this date.”

“This execution presents an authorization token issued by a disjoint authority for this declared scope and window.”

“This receipt can be checked against the authorization token and declared license metadata.”

“No valid token was presented under the declared verification procedure.”

“This evidence may support contractual or legal review.”

---

## 16. Unsafe Claims

Unsafe claims include:

“This proves the other system copied the algorithm.”

“This output correlation proves plagiarism.”

“This Merkle structure proves authorship.”

“This self-signed receipt proves authorized execution.”

“This token proves all executions were licensed.”

“This timestamp proves sole authorship.”

“This validator proves infringement.”

“This system detects unauthorized external reimplementations.”

---

## 17. Adversary Classes

A-DAP’s IP-adjacent evidence must specify the adversary.

### 17.1 Defensible Target — Licensee Exceeding Scope

A licensee uses an authorized deployment outside the declared scope.

A-DAP may help if execution receipts and authorization tokens are externally issued or independently reconcilable.

### 17.2 Defensible Target — Partner Misreporting Usage

A partner reports fewer executions than actually occurred.

A-DAP may help if token issuance, receipt submission, or usage reconciliation is independently observable.

### 17.3 Weak Target — Honest Licensee Misconfiguration

A licensee accidentally uses an outdated version, wrong scope, or expired window.

A-DAP may help detect inconsistency.

### 17.4 Not Defensible by Cryptography Alone — External Reimplementation

A competitor independently implements a similar method without using the licensed engine, code, keys, or authorization channel.

A-DAP cannot cryptographically prove copying.

### 17.5 Not Defensible by Output Correlation Alone — Similar Behavior

A third-party system produces highly similar outputs.

That may support investigation, but it is not proof of origin.

---

## 18. Diagnostic Questions

Before using A-DAP for IP-adjacent evidence, ask:

1. What exactly is being proven?
2. Is the claim temporal priority, authorized execution, or plagiarism?
3. Who controls the signing key?
4. Who controls the authorization token issuer?
5. Can the licensee issue its own “authorized” receipts?
6. Can the licensee suppress receipts?
7. Can tokens be replayed or copied?
8. Are tokens bound to deployment, version, scope, and time?
9. Can token issuance be verified outside the licensee?
10. Can execution completeness be reconciled?
11. Does the evidence prove existence, or does it claim authorship?
12. Does similarity evidence prove copying, or only resemblance?
13. What human/legal evidence is needed beyond A-DAP artifacts?

---

## 19. Business Use Without Overclaim

A defensible business use is:

- release anchoring;
- license metadata anchoring;
- authorized deployment receipts;
- token-based execution windows;
- usage reconciliation;
- audit packages for licensed deployments;
- contractual evidence for scope compliance;
- timestamped governance logs.

A dangerous business use is:

- automated plagiarism detection;
- behavioral watermarking as proof;
- output correlation as infringement evidence;
- self-signed authorization receipts;
- universal piracy detection.

The first category is evidence infrastructure.

The second category is overclaim.

---

## 20. Summary

A-DAP can help prove temporal priority.

But temporal priority is not sole authorship.

A-DAP can help support authorized-execution evidence.

But authorized execution cannot be self-attested by the engine being checked.

A-DAP cannot prove algorithmic plagiarism from output similarity alone.

A-DAP cannot force external reimplementers to participate in its token system.

The defensible position is:

A-DAP supports existence-by-date and externally authorized execution evidence.

It does not prove copying, authorship, or infringement by itself.
